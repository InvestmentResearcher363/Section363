In Distress: The Recent Bout of Financial Restructurings in the Oil and Gas Industry

Sukh Dhillon

 

Scope and Definitions

            For the purpose of this paper, the scope of study will be the U.S. oil & gas industry over the last five years (2014-2019) and the period immediately prior that led to the current state of the industry. A distressed business enterprise, as the term will be used in this paper, will be limited to those oil & gas enterprises exhibiting either insolvency, whether that be technical insolvency or insolvency in a bankruptcy sense, or default, whether that be technical default or legal default. Technical insolvency occurs when a firm temporarily cannot meet current debt obligations due to a lack of liquidity, whereas insolvency in a bankruptcy sense chronically afflicts distressed firms due to a firm’s negative fundamental net worth position, in which total liabilities exceed a fair valuation of total assets (Altman and Hotchkiss 5).

This paper will attempt to analyze the financial restructuring activity this subset of enterprises chose to pursue in the time period of interest in response to their respective corporate distress. Financial restructuring, as it will be used in this paper, will refer to either distressed restructurings, in which debtor firms continue day-to-day operations while coordinating said restructuring with their creditors in order to avert a bankruptcy filing; prepackaged Chapter 11 filings, in which debtor firms settle on a restructuring plan with a required portion of claimholders and then file for bankruptcy; or a regular bankruptcy filing, either Chapter 7 for liquidation or Chapter 11 for a bankruptcy reorganization with the goal to reemerge as a going concern post-bankruptcy (Altman and Hotchkiss 5-6).

Background

            As in any study, one must correctly identify the start of the narrative or trend of interest. This narrative begins well before the 2015-2019 period of interest; one has to begin this investigation with the most recent global commodities super cycle. This commodities super cycle was driven by the rapid speed with which China, India, and other emerging market economies were industrializing and urbanizing, thereby increasing global aggregated demand for commodities (Erten and Ocampo 14). As a result of these substantial forces of increasing demand, the real prices of energy and metals commodities more than doubled between 2003 and 2008 (Erten and Ocampo 14). Even after the global economy slowed down during the Global Financial Crisis (GFC), however, commodity prices began recovering remarkably quickly (Erten and Ocampo 14). This resulted in a renewal of this commodities super cycle from 2010 to 2012 (Erten and Ocampo 14). Hence, this most recent commodities super cycle, which included the price of oil, led to a nearly decade-long increase in prices with only a 2-year interruption in the middle of the cycle (Erten and Ocampo 14).

            As argued by Erten and Ocampo, the presence of commodity price super cycles matters for a number of reasons. First off, commodity price trends have been long thought to be central policy issues for developing nations whose economies depend upon the production of these very commodities (Erten and Ocampo 14). Furthermore, since within these individual commodity-producing industries decisions to increase or decrease production capacity directly relate to expected future prices and profits and investment projects to increase production may take several years to develop, firms within these industries must pay the utmost attention to medium-term price trends in order to optimize their investment decisions (Erten and Ocampo 14). Lastly, and arguably most importantly for this paper, financial investors in these industries utilize substantial commodity price increases as a way to guard against, and therefore justify, potential downside risks in portfolio management (Erten and Ocampo 14).

            It is this final point that will come to be incredibly important in telling the narrative of the U.S. oil & gas industry over the last five years. The commodities super cycle of the past decade, and the resultant substantial increase in oil prices that this super cycle brought, would become a critical tool deployed by investors to justify increasing investment in the U.S. oil & gas industry. One group of investors that bears critical analysis in order to understand the phenomenon of investment into the U.S. oil & gas industry is the collective group of private equity investors. Before delving into the role that private equity investors played, however, it would be helpful to take a quick aside in order to explain the changing role that private equity has come to play in the U.S. financial system since the GFC.

            Prior to the GFC, private equity investors played a much smaller role in the U.S. financial system. From the early days of the leveraged buyout pioneers Henry Kravis and George Roberts, of the firm KKR they founded in the 1970s, the private equity industry has mainly concentrated on this leveraged buyout investment model of buying up whole companies with massive amounts of debt, otherwise known as leverage, and utilizing the debt’s low interest rate and their substantial cost cutting efforts to drive returns that would be realized upon ultimately selling these companies for higher prices than they originally paid for them (Vandevelde, “How the biggest private equity firms became the new banks”). Over the last decade, however, private equity investors have dramatically expanded from their original leveraged buyout investment model. Now the biggest private equity investment outfits have dramatically expanded to add private debt to their investment portfolios and, in the process, have become a part of a new class of lightly regulated asset managers that have filled the investment voids created by the U.S. banks stepping back from more risky deals that they have been prevented from taking part in by the post-GFC Dodd-Frank legislation (Vandevelde, “How the biggest private equity firms became the new banks”).

            In contrast to the banks, which are financial institutions that depend on deposits and other short-term funding liabilities to invest in long-term assets, private equity firms raise their capital from long-term investors, such as institutional investors like insurance companies and pension funds (Vandevelde, “How the biggest private equity firms became the new banks”). As part of the aforementioned Dodd-Frank legislation that was created to ensure the banks prioritize more prudent risk management and take greater care of depositors’ money, banks have been forced to recapitalize their balance sheets and build larger capital cushions (Vandevelde, “How the biggest private equity firms became the new banks”). These regulatory requirements, combined with a weak recovery following the GFC, have led to banks not really extending a great deal of credit (Vandevelde, “How the biggest private equity firms became the new banks”). This phenomenon, in turn, served as a welcome beckoning call to private equity investors.

            Three of the four biggest U.S. private equity firms, which include Blackstone, Apollo, and KKR, now oversee more money in their credit funds than they do in their private equity arms (Vandevelde, “How the biggest private equity firms became the new banks”). This is a substantial difference from where the industry stood a decade ago, when debt funds accounted for around only one-fifth of their assets under management (Vandevelde, “How the biggest private equity firms became the new banks”). Questions have been raised, as a result of this dramatic increase in private credit. In particular, the question of whether certain lending risks have simply been relocated to another, less tightly regulated area of the market.

The Lead-Up

            With this aside delving into private equity investors completed, it is now time to turn to the behavior of these investors immediately prior to the 2014-2019 period of interest for the U.S. oil & gas industry. A critical piece of evidence that sheds light on the behavior of private equity investors at this time is a 2013 Ernst & Young white paper titled “Financing the future energy landscape: Private equity trends in oil and gas.” This piece of literature produced by the professional services firm first touches on the state of the private equity industry. One of the key highlights of this private equity industry section include an emphasis that the industry is in the middle of a period of great transformation, echoing some of the aforementioned characterization of private equity growing into a larger role within the financial system over the past decade (Ernst & Young 4).

Moreover, the white paper emphasizes how private equity investors are again on the offensive and how the industry is developing its business model to align with the new parameters of the investment world (Ernst & Young 4). The section specifically outlines how these business model developments involve private equity investors expanding beyond their traditional mold of leveraged buyouts in favor of reinventing themselves as fully diversified alternative asset managers with private equity, venture capital, debt and credit, hedge fund, and fund-of-fund investment platforms (Ernst & Young 4). All of the above depicted in this white paper from 2013 confirm all that hindsight has shown about the changing role of private equity over the last decade. These private equity industry changes, however, do not even get at the most critical information that this white paper provides. This critical information is the manner in which this piece of literature, and by proxy private equity investors overall, characterized the oil & gas industry in 2013.

The white paper characterizes the industry as both being on a never-ending pursuit for resources to meet increasing energy demand and undergoing substantial technological development through the industry’s development of drilling and well completion technologies (Ernst & Young 4). The specifics of this technological development, it turns out, are very important and will be analyzed in a later section of this paper. Owing to the white paper’s glowing portrayal of the oil & gas industry, it should come as no surprise that the document concludes that the industry is an appealing space for private equity investment (Ernst & Young 4). This white paper also presents objective market data that illustrate that private equity investors more broadly shared this rosy view of the oil & gas industry. The market data that this piece of literature also presents is private equity activity in the oil and gas industry from the year 2008 to 2012.

Although the numbers the white paper presents are for the global industry, for the purposes of this argument, they can additionally be taken as a proxy of the U.S. oil & gas industry. The industry registered $18.4 billion of investment over 65 deals in 2008, $3.6 billion over 34 deals in 2009, $8.3 billion over 51 deals in 2010, $23.2 billion over 45 deals in 2011, and $27.2 billion over 56 deals in 2012 (Ernst & Young 4). Interestingly, the timeline of this investment activity pairs up remarkably well with the timeline of the commodities super cycle that Erten and Ocampo discuss. Specifically, the negative effect of the GFC on commodity, and specifically oil, prices can be seen in the drastic decline in investment in the industry in 2009 and the subsequent rise in prices in from 2010 to 2012 reflects the resurgence of investment in the industry with the largest investment year during this five-year period taking place in 2012.

It should, therefore, come as no surprise that, in a survey sent to 100 private equity executives, one of the main reasons the white paper identified as an influence on private equity investor involvement in the oil & gas industry was commodity prices, with 17% of respondents listing it as a main driver (Ernst & Young 6). That this survey found a significant amount of sampled private equity practitioners were willing to admit that their decision to invest in the oil & gas industry was at least partly influenced by the role of prices reveals a startling takeaway. This takeaway is that the global oil & gas industry, and, for the purposes of this argument, the U.S. oil & gas industry by extension, received at least some private equity investment not on the basis of fundamental value or some other value-dependent investment process, but rather on the speculative basis of price and, as this was a rising-price environment at the time, on the basis of rising prices. This demonstrates the final principle that Erten and Ocampo discuss: financial investors utilize substantial commodity price increases to protect themselves from the downside risks associated with inferior portfolio management.

As it turns out, not only were the private equity investors utilizing rising prices to justify investment. The U.S. oil & gas companies themselves were also utilizing the rising prices to usher in an era of expansion. This also is where the technology discussion comes back into play. Despite the oil & gas industry’s knowledge of a substantial shale oil resource’s existence in the U.S. for several decades, the resource could not be utilized due to the industry lacking the technology facilitating production from the source rock itself (Azar 6). It was only after the innovation of combining hydraulic fracturing with horizontal drilling that the oil in nonporous shale became within the U.S. oil & gas industry’s technical reach (Azar 6). The process and the technology behind it, however, remain quite capital-intensive to this day (Azar 6). It thus stands to reason that the true spark of the shale technology revolution that the Ernst & Young white paper referenced was in fact the era of dramatically low capital costs that interest rates being drastically cut following the GFC created (Azar 6).

As demonstrated by the combination of the above discussions of the low capital cost situation that was favorable for capital investment, the rising oil-price environment, and the full-scale arrival of private equity investors on the U.S. investment scene, the stage had been set immediately following 2008 for the perfect scenario for the U.S. oil & gas industry. With the GFC now behind the U.S. economy, oil prices rising to above $100 per barrel, and private equity ready to pounce on it all, billions of dollars of investment flowed into shale over the six-year period following the GFC in 2008 (Azar 6). This investment came overwhelmingly in the form of low-cost debt, with a significant portion coming from the aforementioned outgrowth of private equity investors wading into the world of private credit (Azar 6). This widespread loosening of the credit faucet allowed North American exploration & production companies (E&Ps) to tap into high-yield bond markets in a heavily accelerating fashion between the years 2009 and 2012 (Azar 6-7).

This industry-wide credit binge progressed unchecked for a few years and it certainly had its side effects. Propelled by the low-interest environment, while U.S. E&P debt climbed to a historically high level, interest costs in proportion to this debt fell (Azar 11). Between the years 2005 and 2015, aggregate E&P debt ballooned 300 percent from $50 billion to $200 billion (Azar 11). While this was occurring, however, interest expense only rose 150 percent from $4 billion to  $10 billion. These favorable conditions, in which debt rose twice as rapidly as the cost of this capital rose, translated into an overall decline in borrowing cost (Azar 11). In addition to this, however, the ability to have access to low-cost debt pushed the industry into a mode of outspend its generated cash flow to fund growth projects mainly with new debt rather than a more sustainable combination of debt and equity financing (Azar 12). This, in turn, demonstrates how low interest rates propagated an industry-wide shift to both raise debt levels in order to boost return to equity and prioritize the pursuit of growth and investment over cash flow generation (Azar 11-12).

When the Music Stopped

This debt-fueled party, however, had to come to an end eventually. This end came in the second half of 2014. The price of oil plummeted from above $90 per barrel in the third quarter of 2014 to $50 per barrel in the fourth quarter (Azar 12). The genesis of this price shock came from OPEC. The cartel, attempting to flex its muscles against the encroaching threat of U.S. shale oil, made its push to defend market share by simply flooding the market (Azar 12).  To do so, it elected to forgo oil supply cuts and ended up successfully dictating the market price (Azar 12). This low-price oil environment, in turn, had a brutal impact on non-investment grade North American E&Ps (Azar 14). To understand the mechanisms through which the North American E&Ps felt this negative impact, however, require a deeper understanding of their nuanced debt financing instruments (Azar 14).

The financing instrument of particular interest in the case of analyzing the effect felt by North American E&Ps in late 2014’s low-oil-price environment is the reserve base lending (RBL) structure (Azar 14). As opposed to their investment-grade brethren that were afforded easy access to the robust debt and equity markets to fund their growth prospects, non-investment grade E&Ps, on the other hand, were forced to use a combination of equity and debt under their respective RBL facilities to fund their capital expenditure (Azar 14). RBLs are in essence bank-syndicated revolving credit facilities secured by the company’s proved oil and gas reserves (Azar 14). More broadly, RBLs are a form of asset-backed financing commonly utilized by the oil & gas industry. In terms of what constitutes “proved reserves,” the asset is subdivided into three categories, each with its own level of associated risk tied up in production and valuation: proved developed producing (PDP), proved developed nonproducing (PDNP), and proved undeveloped (PUD) (Azar 14).

            As “producing” reserves implies such reserves currently generate some measurable level of oil & gas resource output, risk associated with the production and valuation of these reserves is the lowest of the three and, as a result, the Advance Rate risk factor for these reserves ranges from 95 to 99 percent (Azar 14).  PDNP reserves have an Advance Rate risk factor between 65 and 75 percent; and, as PUD reserves carry the additional capital expenditure/development risk, they have an Advance Rate risk factor of 50 to 60 percent (Azar 14). Typically, however, risk factors associated with each category of proved deserves are, within a bank’s internal policy, of course, at the discretion of a bank’s independent reserve and production engineer (Azar 14).  The independent engineer is an individual a given bank engages in order to provide due diligence on said bank’s calculations for determining a debtor’s borrowing base, which itself is a function of the company’s proved reserves (Azar 14). The critical point in all of this is that the borrowing base calculation is conducted based upon the net present value of future cash flows from the debtor’s oil & gas reserves under each lender’s price assumptions (Azar 14).

            Once the low-price environment descended upon the U.S. oil & gas industry in late 2014, although some banks, through the conservatism of their price assumptions prior to the oil price crash, were able to provide lending support to their borrowers, the potential borrowing bases of many non-investment grade North American E&Ps shrunk dramatically due to the market, which also performs this hypothetical net present value calculation based upon pricing, shutting them out of the market for high-yield debt (Azar 19). As a result of new debt issuance all but disappearing for non-investment grade North American E&Ps, their entire business model had collapsed. They were already highly levered and, as a result, were reliant on debt financing to fund their operations (Azar 19). They had become victims to the class corporate finance problem of debt overhang, in which a company becomes overleveraged and out of favor with the market (Azar 19). As a result of their credit faucet being abruptly closed, a wave of bankruptcies ensued.

Cue the Restructuring

            As a result of the post-GFC environment of low interest rates, an incentive had spread across the North American E&P industry to dial up the leverage and risk. The scenario in which this incentive to increase leverage and risk runs unchecked is known under the economic theory of risk management as asset substitution and it results from shareholders’ claims on the firm acting as an effective option on the total market value of the firm and shareholders, as a result, wanting to increase their expected return by increasing the firm’s total risk (Duffie and Singleton 18). By 2014, as this asset substitution had run amok through the industry, capital structures had been constructed with no heed paid to the possibility of falling prices. In late 2014, after prices began trending in the opposite direction from which they had been going for the better part of a decade, these capital structures were no longer sustainable. As a result, the number of newly distressed enterprises meandering along in the sea of North American E&Ps skyrocketed.

This can be seen in the wave of bankruptcy filings over the four-year period from 2015 to 2018. More than 100 North American E&Ps filed for bankruptcy in the first two years of 2015 and 2016, with 44 filing in 2015 and 70 filing in 2016 (Haynes and Boone 2).  The next two years saw a substantial decrease in the number of filings, with 24 filings in 2017 and 29 in 2018 (Haynes and Boone 2). Summing the total filings for each year, the four-year period saw 167 North American E&Ps file bankruptcy (Haynes and Boone 2). In comparison to the telecom sector-wide wave of bankruptcies from 2001 to 2003, in which 43 companies filed for bankruptcy, this wave of North American E&P filings is massive (Keisman and Marshella, “Moody's: 2015 E&P sector defaults, creditors losses on track to significantly exceed historical sector averages”). It is also critical to remember that, although helpful, the number of bankruptcy filings does not provide a complete picture of financial distress within the U.S. oil & gas industry during this time period because it does not account for one type of financial restructuring, the out-of-court distressed restructuring.

Departing from looking at the sheer size of this wave of restructurings, it is also critical to analyze what this wave accomplished. In a third-quarter 2018 analysis of the global oil and natural gas industry, EIA analyzed data from 106 public oil and gas companies (EIA 3). The analysis, although global, can be considered somewhat representative of the U.S. industry as well because 75 of the 106 companies are U.S.-based (EIA 3). The analysis found that energy companies’ free cash flow, or the difference between cash from operations and capital expenditure, was $149 billion for the four-quarter period ending September 30, 2018, the largest four-quarter sum over the five years from 2013 to 2018, and that the companies had decreased debt for eight consecutive quarters, giving way to the lowest long-term debt-to-equity ratio since third-quarter 2014 (EIA 2). Although this sounds impressive, it is also important to remember that decreasing debt-to-equity to a level in line with late 2014 is not that remarkable given that the North American E&Ps in 2014 were at a historically high aggregate leverage level (Azar 14). That being said, however, it is positive to see the period of restructuring has brought the industry to a point at which it has consistently been diligently reducing debt and sustainably prioritizing cash flow generation over its previous priorities of investment/capital expenditure and growth for the sheer purpose of growth.

Conclusion and Future Research

The U.S. oil & gas industry from 2014 to 2019 and the period immediately preceding this five-year period was characterized by times of great success and subsequent hard times. That being said, however, the period of restructuring in the U.S. oil & gas industry between 2015 and 2018 may have been a net positive in the midst of the industry’s troubles as it allowed the North American E&Ps to utilize Ch. 11 of the U.S. Bankruptcy Code as a useful tool to correct balance sheets (WRDIC 2019 “Sparking Transformation: Energy Restructuring Panel”). Although more work needs to be done across the industry to further diminish debt to even below 2014 levels, the financial restructurings of the last few years have provided a good first step in the right direction away from risky and unsustainable leverage and towards becoming a prudent industry that prizes cash flow generation above all else. Areas of future research may involve looking into prognoses for levels of future financial distress (or lack thereof) within the U.S. oil & gas industry upon factoring the changing demand for fossil fuels in a world gradually moving towards renewable energy and electrification.
